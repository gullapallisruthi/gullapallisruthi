Are South Asian Adults in the U.S. Aware of Cardiovascular Risk Factors?

An exploration of the "Weekend Warrior" paradox, the immigrant experience, and the critical gap in preventive screening.

By Sruthi Gullapalli • Feb 2026 • ActiveSTEM.org

Throughout the world, people often believe that exercise is purely beneficial, but in my South Asian community, I have started to notice a troubling pattern. I see many adults who are laser-focused on their careers and families, yet often neglect their health until they are much older. This delay is why we are seeing so many tragic heart events in our community.

In the article "South Asians and Cardiovascular Risk," researchers highlight how people from the Indian subcontinent have a higher risk of heart disease at younger ages. This scares me because I see uncles and fathers starting intense sports like cricket without knowing their baseline health status. Sudden cardiac events are becoming alarmingly common in recreational sports, demonstrating how dangerous it can be to start exercising without a check-up.

“We wouldn't drive a car without checking the engine, so why do we push our bodies without checking our hearts?”

Origin of Inquiry

My interest in South Asian cardiovascular health did not begin with statistics or journal articles—it began with observation. Within many immigrant families, health is often treated as something to worry about later. This mindset allows silent conditions such as hypertension and insulin resistance to progress unnoticed.

The topic became urgent after a recent death in my family. A relative who seemed physically fit traveled from the U.S. to India and suffered heart failure shortly after arrival. This tragedy revealed how cardiovascular risk is not only biological but also shaped by migration, culture, and environment. These experiences led me to ask: Why do South Asian immigrants face such disproportionate rates of heart failure?

The "South Asian Phenotype"

One specific biological issue is the "South Asian Phenotype." Although many people look healthy on the outside, they might have high visceral fat around their organs. Medical literature shows South Asians have a genetic predisposition to insulin resistance, meaning standard measurements like BMI can be deceptive. A person might have a normal weight but dangerous cholesterol levels—a condition often called "skinny fat."

The BMI Trap: A Case Study (Hypothetical)

Consider "Raj," a profile that is increasingly common in our community. His standard metrics suggest health, but a deeper look reveals high cardiac risk.

Profile:
• Age: 42
• BMI: 24 (Normal)
• Non-smoker
Hidden Risks:
• High Triglycerides Risk
• Low HDL (Good Cholesterol) Risk
• Sedentary Job Risk

Conclusion: If Raj starts a high-intensity interval training program without screening, his "normal" BMI offers zero protection against a cardiac event.

The Weekend Warrior Paradox

This biological risk collides with the "Weekend Warrior" phenomenon. Adults who have been sedentary for years suddenly decide to play intense sports. Immigrants in the U.S. work incredibly hard to establish themselves, often neglecting exercise for decades. When they finally decide to "get healthy" in their 40s, they jump into high-intensity activities. Without prior screening, this sudden strain on a compromised cardiovascular system can be catastrophic.

Preserving Culture, Protecting Health

It is important to note that addressing these risks does not mean erasing our culture. South Asian traditions have immense strengths—our emphasis on family meals creates strong social support systems, which are vital for mental health. Traditional ingredients like turmeric and ginger have potent anti-inflammatory properties.

The goal is modification, not elimination. We can still enjoy traditional flavors while adjusting the preparation methods—using less oil, reducing the portion of white rice in favor of fiber-rich vegetables, and recognizing that "home-cooked" doesn't automatically mean "heart-healthy" if the glycemic load is high. By blending modern medical awareness with cultural respect, we can protect our heritage and our hearts simultaneously.

Conclusion

We have a moral obligation to protect our community. Exercise is vital, but we cannot treat it as a cure-all without understanding the vessel we are exercising. By encouraging screenings for lipid profiles and insulin resistance before hitting the cricket pitch or treadmill, we can ensure that our efforts to get fit result in longer, healthier lives—not preventable tragedies.
